Road Safety Risks

Understanding the dangers we all face on the road

1.35 Million

people die each year in road traffic crashes worldwide

Every road user faces unique risks. Let's explore how we can all stay safer.

Risks for Drivers

üì± Distracted Driving

Using mobile phones, eating, or adjusting controls while driving takes your attention away from the road. Even a 2-second distraction at 60 km/h means traveling 33 meters blindly.

‚ö° Speeding

Higher speeds reduce reaction time and increase crash severity. A 5% increase in speed leads to a 10% increase in crashes causing injuries.

üò¥ Fatigue

Driving tired is like driving drunk. Drowsy drivers have slower reaction times and impaired judgment, making them 3 times more likely to crash.

üç∫ Impaired Driving

Alcohol and drugs severely affect coordination, judgment, and reaction time. Even small amounts can be deadly.

Risks for Passengers

üî¥ Not Wearing Seatbelts

Seatbelts reduce the risk of death by 45% for front-seat passengers and 44% for rear-seat passengers. Unbelted passengers become projectiles in crashes.

üë∂ Improper Child Restraints

Children need age-appropriate car seats. Incorrectly installed or wrong-sized seats offer little protection. Car crashes are a leading cause of child deaths.

üöô Overcrowding

Too many passengers reduce driver visibility, distract the driver, and mean fewer people can use safety restraints properly.

‚ö†Ô∏è Distracting the Driver

Loud talking, arguments, or sudden movements can divert the driver's attention at critical moments, increasing crash risk.

üö¥

Risks for Cyclists

üëÅÔ∏è Poor Visibility

Cyclists are smaller and harder to see, especially at night or in bad weather. Many crashes occur when drivers simply don't see cyclists.

üõ£Ô∏è Road Positioning

Riding too close to parked cars (door zone) or hugging the curb puts cyclists in danger. Proper lane positioning is crucial for safety.

ü™ñ No Helmet

Head injuries are the leading cause of cyclist deaths. Helmets reduce the risk of head injury by 60% and brain injury by 58%.

üöß Poor Road Infrastructure

Lack of bike lanes, potholes, and debris force cyclists into dangerous situations, mixing with fast-moving traffic.

Risks for Pillion Passengers

ü™ñ Not Wearing Helmets

Pillion passengers are equally vulnerable to head injuries. Helmets are as critical for passengers as they are for riders. Always wear a properly fitted, certified helmet.

ü§ù Poor Holding Technique

Not holding on properly or holding onto loose clothing instead of the rider or grab rails can cause passengers to fall off during sudden stops or turns.

‚öñÔ∏è Balance Issues

Sudden movements or leaning incorrectly can destabilize the motorcycle. Passengers must move with the bike and rider, not against them.

üëü Exposed Body Parts

Wearing shorts, sandals, or loose clothing leaves skin exposed to severe injuries in crashes. Proper protective gear is essential.

How to Stay Safe

  • Always wear seatbelts (drivers & passengers) and helmets (riders & pillion)
  • Never use your phone while driving - pull over if you must use it
  • Follow speed limits and adjust speed for conditions
  • Never drive under the influence of alcohol or drugs
  • Make yourself visible - use lights, reflectors, and bright clothing
  • Get enough rest before long journeys
  • Use proper child car seats for children
  • Maintain your vehicle regularly for safe operation
  • Be predictable - signal your intentions to other road users
  • Stay alert and anticipate dangerous situations
